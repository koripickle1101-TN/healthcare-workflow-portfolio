# Case Study: Revenue Cycle Workflow Breakdown

**Project Type:** Self-directed portfolio case study  
**Role Simulated:** RCM Workflow Analyst  
**Focus Area:** Patient access, claims readiness, denial prevention, workflow validation  
**Brand System:** White background, black text, Vols Orange `#FF8200` accents

## Executive Summary

This case study analyzes how revenue cycle failures can begin upstream before a claim is submitted. The purpose is to demonstrate practical RCM workflow thinking, not to claim employer or client experience.

The analysis follows a simple operational question:

**Where does the workflow allow preventable errors to move forward?**

The case study identifies front-end breakdown points, maps downstream impact, and proposes validation checkpoints that could reduce avoidable rework and denial risk.

---

## Scenario

A healthcare organization is experiencing recurring claim issues related to eligibility, authorization, and documentation readiness.

Instead of treating each denial as a separate billing problem, this case study evaluates the full workflow from patient intake to claim submission.

---

## Problem Statement

Revenue cycle breakdowns often appear at the billing or denial stage, but many root causes begin earlier in the workflow.

Common upstream risk points include:

- Incomplete patient demographic information
- Incorrect or outdated insurance details
- Missing eligibility verification
- Authorization requirements not confirmed before service
- Documentation that does not fully support the claim
- Weak handoffs between patient access, clinical documentation, and billing

These issues can create downstream rework, delayed reimbursement, avoidable denials, and patient confusion.

## Workflow Map

```text

   ↓
Insurance Capture
   ↓
Eligibility Verification
   ↓
Authorization Review
   ↓
Documentation Check
   ↓
Claim Preparation
   ↓
Claim Submission
   ↓
Payment / Denial / Follow-Up
```

---

## Key Risk Analysis

| Workflow Stage | Potential Breakdown | Downstream Impact | Prevention Opportunity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Patient Intake | Missing demographics or incorrect patient information | Rework, claim edits, patient contact delays | Required fields before account moves forward |
| Insurance Capture | Wrong payer, inactive coverage, outdated plan | Eligibility denial risk | Payer and plan validation before service |
| Eligibility Verification | No validation before service | Preventable claim rejection or denial | Verify coverage close to service date |
| Authorization Review | Requirement unclear or missed | Authorization denial risk | Confirm payer-specific rules before visit |
| Documentation Check | Missing support for service billed | Documentation-related denial risk | Pre-bill documentation review |
| Claim Preparation | Claim submitted without final validation | Increased denial and rework risk | Claim readiness checklist |

---

## Proposed Workflow Controls

1. Confirm complete patient demographics before account progression.
2. Verify active insurance coverage close to the date of service.
3. Confirm payer-specific authorization requirements.
4. Check that documentation supports the service billed.
5. Review claim readiness before submission.
6. Track denial patterns and send feedback upstream.

## Simulated Improvement Targets

These targets are for educational demonstration only and are not based on employer or client data.

| Metric | Current Risk Signal | Target Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Eligibility-related denials | High repeat risk | Reduce through front-end verification |
| Claim rework | Frequent manual correction | Reduce through validation checkpoints |
| Clean claim readiness | Inconsistent | Improve through pre-submission review |
| Patient follow-up delays | Possible after claim issue | Reduce through accurate intake data |

---

## Analyst Takeaway

The strongest revenue cycle improvements often happen before the claim is submitted.

A denial prevention mindset asks:

- What allowed the error to move forward?
- Which step should have caught it earlier?
- How can the workflow prevent the same issue next time?
